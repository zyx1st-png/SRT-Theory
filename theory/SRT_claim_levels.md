# Claim levels

How to read the different roles a claim can play in SRT. The point is to keep proven, testable, and speculative claims clearly separated — so the strong parts are not inflated, and the exploratory parts are not mistaken for settled results.

| Term | Meaning | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| canonical | The current authoritative source for a definition, variable, or usage rule. | Being canonical *within the theory* does not make it an externally established law of nature. |
| primitive axiom | A minimal starting commitment in the formal core. | Not derived from a bridge or a public summary. |
| constitutive theorem | A core-level theorem generated from the primitive commitments. | Requires formal support; not supplied by analogy. |
| bridge hypothesis | A cross-domain mapping between SRT and another framework. | A bridge hypothesis is not canonical. |
| operational proxy | A measurable or model-facing approximation of an SRT construct. | A proxy is not the construct it approximates. |
| lab hypothesis | A test-facing claim or measurement proposal. | It stays a hypothesis until tested and interpreted. |
| public summary | A reader-facing simplification (like the files in this repo). | A public summary does not upgrade the claim level of what it summarizes. |

## Hard boundaries

- Bridge hypotheses do not define the core.
- Operational proxies do not replace the core variables they approximate.
- Public summaries do not upgrade claim level.
- External convergence — other theories or data agreeing — can strengthen or pressure a claim, but does not bypass its own justification.

The reason to state this explicitly: a theory that ranges across physics, consciousness, AI, and society is constantly tempted to let a vivid analogy stand in for a proof, or to let "it's consistent with X" pass as "it's established." These levels are the discipline that blocks that.
