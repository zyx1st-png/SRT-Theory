# Selective Reality Theory (SRT) — Quick Start

SRT begins from a reversal:

> Reality is not first given and then selected. Reality becomes determinate through constrained selection.
>
> 现实不是先被给定、再被选择；现实是在约束中的选择里变得确定。

Reading time: 8–10 minutes. Difficulty: beginner.

This guide is for new readers. It is a public-facing map, not a final definition source.

## 1. SRT in one sentence

SRT is a selection-first theory of reality.

It asks how possibilities become realities: how something not-yet-determinate becomes actual, stable, shared, costly, meaningful, or binding.

In ordinary language:

> Reality is selected under constraint, stabilized through history, and tested by resistance.

SRT does not say that anything can become real just because someone wants it. A selected reality must survive constraint, pay cost, meet resistance, and leave consequences.

## 2. Why selection comes before stable existence

Most everyday thinking starts with objects:

1. The world is already there.
2. We notice or choose parts of it.
3. Our choices happen inside that already-given world.

SRT reverses the order at a deeper level:

1. There are structured possibilities.
2. Selection anchors some possibilities into determinate manifestation.
3. Repeated and overlapping selections harden into stable reality.

This does not mean "the mind creates the world." It means that determinate reality is not a flat inventory waiting unchanged behind experience. What becomes real has passed through selection, constraint, cost, history, and convergence.

Simple example: before you speak, many possible sentences are available. Once you speak, one sentence becomes actual, it changes the situation, and it leaves consequences. SRT treats this as a small local example of a much broader structure: possibility becomes determinate through constrained selection.

## 3. The three domains: L0, L1, L2

SRT often uses three domains to keep this structure clear.

| Domain | Plain meaning | Quick image |
|---|---|---|
| `L0` | latent possibility | what could become determinate |
| `L1` | manifest selection | what is currently actualized |
| `L2` | stabilized convergence | what has hardened through repeated selections |

### L0: latent possibility

`L0` is not nothingness. It is the domain of possible ways reality could become determinate. You can think of it as the space of live possibilities before one path is selected. It is not directly experienced as a finished object; it is inferred through what can still become actual.

### L1: manifest selection

`L1` is the selected manifestation: the determinate slice that has become actual for a system, situation, or observer-position. This is the level where something happens, appears, is acted on, or becomes the current reality.

### L2: stabilized convergence

`L2` is what hardens when selections repeat, overlap, and become stable. Examples include language, institutions, scientific objects, habits, shared worlds, and social realities. `L2` is powerful because it feels given after enough history, but SRT keeps asking how it was selected, stabilized, and maintained.

Stability is not legitimacy. A pattern can be stable and still be pathological, coercive, false, or costly.

## 4. What is `Ĝθ`?

`Ĝθ` names the embodied selection / anchoring operator. In plain language, it is the way a system turns possibility into manifestation under its own constraints.

- `Ĝ` points to the act or structure of selection.
- `θ` points to the parameters that shape selection: body, history, environment, memory, training, context, or system-state.

A human, a lab instrument, a social institution, and an AI system do not select in the same way. Their `θ` is different. That is why SRT does not treat "selection" as just conscious choice. Selection is broader than attention or intention.

## 5. What is `d-value`?

`d-value` is SRT's name for stake-coupled concern. In ordinary language, it asks: does this selection matter to the system in a way that can return consequences, carry risk, and reshape future possibilities?

A high-capacity system can process many options without having much `d-value` if nothing is really at stake for it. A vulnerable living system may have less raw capacity but more stake because what happens can affect its future, body, relationships, or survival.

This is why `d-value` is not mere preference. Preference can be simulated or swapped. Stake involves consequence return and non-substitutability.

## 6. What is `Ψf`?

`Ψf` names ontological friction: the payability burden of making and maintaining a selected reality. In plain language, it asks: what does it cost to turn a possibility into something stable enough to live in, act on, or maintain?

Cost here is not only money or energy. Depending on the domain, it can include information cost, metabolic burden, cognitive load, social friction, risk, time, or structural resistance.

SRT does not say the best world has zero friction. Living systems need nonzero, payable friction. Too little resistance can become fantasy or instability; too much can become collapse, suffering, or lock-in.

## 7. What is `T_dir`?

`T_dir` names directional readability: how well a system can read the direction of its own selection. In ordinary language, it asks: can the system tell where its own reality-making is going?

Low `T_dir` can feel like confusion, value-hiddenness, stuckness, or being carried by a pattern one cannot read. High `T_dir` does not mean certainty. It means the system has enough directional access to reorient, revise, or take responsibility for its selection path.

## 8. What SRT is not

SRT is easy to misread. These boundaries matter.

| Misreading | SRT boundary |
|---|---|
| "Mind creates reality." | SRT is selection-first, not mind-first. Selection is constrained and tested by resistance. |
| "Truth is relative." | SRT studies how determinate realities stabilize; it does not say every claim is equally valid. |
| "Everything is conscious." | Selection is broad; consciousness is thresholded. |
| "Stable means good." | Stability is not legitimacy. Stable patterns can be harmful or false. |
| "External theories prove SRT." | Comparisons translate; external convergence can strengthen or pressure SRT, not replace its own formal core. |

## 9. Why SRT matters for AI, consciousness, and society

### AI

SRT gives a way to distinguish capability from stake. An AI system may generate language, solve tasks, or model the world without bearing consequences in the same way a living subject does. SRT therefore asks about training-time, inference-time, memory, embodiment, consequence return, and whether any stake-bearing loop exists. This makes SRT useful for AI consciousness debates because it avoids both easy dismissal and easy inflation.

### Consciousness

SRT treats consciousness as thresholded. The key question is not "does everything select?" but "when does selection enter concern, burden, consequence return, self-modulation, and directional readability strongly enough to form a conscious window?" This lets SRT connect philosophy, neuroscience, AI, and phenomenology without saying that all systems are conscious.

### Society

SRT treats institutions, norms, languages, roles, and shared expectations as `L2` structures. They feel real because they stabilize repeated selections. But because stability is not legitimacy, SRT also asks who pays the cost, who can reselect, whose consequences are hidden, and where friction has been externalized.

## 10. A safe first summary

If you remember only one paragraph, remember this:

SRT asks how possibilities become realities. Reality is not first given and then selected; reality becomes determinate through constrained selection. What becomes real must be selected, anchored, paid for, stabilized through history, and tested by resistance. Selection is broader than attention, consciousness is thresholded, and stable reality is not automatically legitimate.
